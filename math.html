pH and pOH Calculations

There are five equations that govern the calculation of pH and pOH. The first, as we already discussed, is that given the hydrogen ion concentration (in molars) [H+] of a solution, pH = -1*log10[H+]. Because exponentiation is the inverse of the logarithm we can also say that given the pH, [H+] (in molars) = 10-pH. This is the second equation. The third and the fourth follow from the fact that the pOH of a solution is (put simply) the pH with hydroxide ions. Given a hydroxide ion concentration (in molars) [OH-] of a solution, pOH = 1*log10[OH-]. Given the pOH, [OH-] (in molars) = 10-pOH. Lastly, the fifth equation is that pH + pOH = 14. Using these five equations, it is possible to ascertain all of the above values given only one.

Example 1

We are given that the hydrogen ion concentration [H+] of a solution is 2.55 * 10-3 molars, and we must find the pH, the pOH, and [OH-].
Firstly, we take the negative log base 10 of the hydrogen ion concentration to find the pH. -log10(2.55 * 10-3) = 2.59, and thus the pH of the solution is 2.59.
Next, to find the pOH of the solution, we subtract the pH from 14. 14 - 2.59 = 11.41, and thus the pOH of the solution is 11.41.
Lastly, we raise ten to the negative pOH to find the concentration of hydroxide ions. 10-11.41 = 3.89 * 10-12, and so the hydroxide ion concentration is 3.89 * 10-12 molars.

Example 2

We are given that the concentration [OH-] of a solution is 4.33 * 10-5 molars, and we must find the pH, the pOH, and [H+].
First, we take the negative log base 10 of the hydroxide ion concentration to find the pOH. -log10(4.33 * 10-3) = 4.63.
Secondly, we subtract the pOH from 14 to find the pH. 14 - 4.63 = 9.37, and thus the pH of the solution is 9.37.
Thirdly, we raise ten to the negative pH to find the hydrogen ion concentration. 10-9.37 = 4.27 * 10-10, and thus the hydrogen ion concentration is 4.27 * 10-10 molars.

Example 3

We are given that the pH of a solution is 7, and we must find the pOH, [H+], and [OH-].
Firstly, to find the pOH of the solution, we subtract the pH from 14. 14 - 7 = 7, and thus the pOH of the solution is 7.
Then, we raise ten to the negative pH to find the concentration of hydrogen ions. 10-7 = 10-7 (surprisingly), and thus the hydrogen ion concentration is 10-7 molars.
Finally, we raise ten to the negative pOH to find the concentration of hydroxide ions. 10-7 = 10-7 (again surprisingly), and thus the hydroxide ion concentration is 10-7 molars.
