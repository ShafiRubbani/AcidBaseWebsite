Classification Terminology

The terms acid and base are used to classify substances with certain chemical properties, often opposite. (One can also use acidic and basic respectively.) This terminology is particularly useful due to the ubiquity of acids and bases in both industrial and household applications. In the industrial realm, acids or bases can be found in fertilizers, cleaners, batteries, explosives, and certain manufacturing processes. In everyday life, we see acids and bases in our cleaning products, food, and even our own biological systems. Acids and bases are defined through three theories, which we will cover in the next section.

Acids

Acids are formed through the reaction of a nonmetal oxide and water. They generally are sour tasting, although one should NEVER TASTE-TEST AN UNKWOWN SUBSTANCE. Acids can act as electrolytes, or in other words, conduct electricity well. They react corrosively with metals, but can also corrode other substances. Some examples of common industrial acids include nitric acid and sulphuric acid, which can be used in fertilizers, paints, and explosives. Examples of common household acids include citric acid and hydrochloric acid, which can be found in citrus fruits and digestive juices respectively. These properties paint a general picture of acidic substances, and we will define them more technically in the next section.

Bases

Bases are created through the reaction of a metal oxide and water, directly contrasting the formation of acids. Bases exist in two types: those soluble in water, and those that are not. The later are referred to merely as bases, while the former are differentiated by the term “alkali.” Bases generally taste bitter, although again one should NEVER TASTE-TEST AN UNKWOWN SUBSTANCE. They have a slippery texture similar to soap, although one should NEVER TOUCH AN UNKWOWN SUBSTANCE. Like acids, alkalis are electrolytes and can conduct electricity well. They too react corrosively with metals, and can also corrode other substances. Common household alkalis include soaps, detergents, and even human blood. In the industrial realm, one can find alkalis in cleaners, as well as some alkali batteries. The information above may allow one to ascertain whether a substance is a base based on its properties, more formal definitions will be provided in the next section.
